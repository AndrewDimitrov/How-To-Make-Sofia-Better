Cleaner air for a brighter future: Improving Sofia’s air quality

How bad is air quality in Sofia?

Air pollution has become one of the most pressing environmental concerns in the world, with Sofia frequently ranking among the cities with the dirtiest air in Europe. In 2024, Sofia continued to experience frequent spikes in pollution levels, with PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ levels exceeding EU and WHO(World Health Organization) limits for safe breathing conditions.

Pollution from heating, traffic, and industrial activities remains a constant challenge, particularly during the winter months. Despite awareness and some efforts to reduce the issue, these harmful pollution levels persist.

More than 8,500 people die prematurely each year from diseases caused by air pollution in Bulgaria. This is more than those who die in car accidents, according to the World Health Organization.

Why is air quality bad in Sofia?

Sofia's air pollution is a result from a combination of geographical and human factors. The city's location in a valley limits air circulation, causing pollutants to linger.

One major contributor to poor air quality is residential heating. Many households rely on burning wood and coal, which releases high levels of fine particulate matter(PM₂.₅).

In addition to this, heavy traffic, particularly from older diesel-powered vehicles, significantly raises levels of nitrogen dioxide(NO₂) and fine particulate matter. Those factors culminate in frequent air quality warnings and pose potential long-term health risks.

What is being done to improve air quality

In recent years, Sofia has taken steps to address the air quality issues by promoting cleaner energy and transportation. The city has introduced electric buses to its public transport system, with plans to expand it further.

Additionally, restrictions have been implemented on using solid fuels like coal for residential heating, while subsidies are available to help households transition to cleaner alternatives. To further combat pollution, Sofia is also focusing on expanding green spaces, which help absorb pollutants and improve the overall urban environment.

What more can be done to improve air quality

Improving Sofia's air quality can start with tracking the main pollutants like PM₂.₅, NO₂, and O₃ to find out which areas are most polluted. Installing sensors across the city will provide live data, showing where the pollution is worst and helping figure out why it’s happening, whether due to traffic, home heating, or factories.

Once the causes are clear, we can create specific solutions like switching to cleaner heating methods, encouraging public transport, and adding more trees and parks. Stronger rules to stop pollution, promoting electric cars, and building bike paths will also reduce emissions.

Air corridors, or open spaces that let air move through the city, can help clear away pollution. By planning spaces between buildings and planting trees along these paths, the city can reduce pollution, lower temperatures, and create healthier, greener areas for people.
